                            <h2>
                                We drink coffee and beer because we like the way they make us feel, a new study shows                            </h2><br><br>
                            <div itemprop="articlebody">
                                
                                <p>&nbsp;Fancy yourself <span style="line-height:2em">a coffee connoisseur with a love for dark roasts? Or maybe hoppy pale ales are more your thing?</span></p>

<p>The truth may be that our preferences for caffeine or alcoholic beverages &ndash; or indeed sugary sodas &ndash; derive not so much from the way they taste but how they make us feel, according to a new study by genetic scientists at Northwestern University that was published in Human Molecular Genetics on Thursday.</p><p>
In her latest work,Marilyn Cornelis, who has published previously on the genetics of coffee consumption, set out to determine which taste genes are responsible for what we drink.</p><!-- yenko in read content --> <div id="grf_nationmultimediacom_6" style="clear:both"></div> <!-- end yenko in read content -->

                                <p>
But to her and the team&rsquo;s surprise, people&rsquo;s preferences weren&rsquo;t based on variations in taste genes but rather the genes that are related to the beverages&rsquo; mind-altering effects.</p><p>
&ldquo;The genetics underlying our preferences are related to the psychoactive components of these drinks,&rdquo; Cornelis notes.</p><p>
&ldquo;People like the way coffee and alcohol make them feel. That&rsquo;s why they drink it.&rdquo;</p><p>
The American Heart Association and National Institutes of Health-funded study collected 24-hour dietary questionnaires from some 336,000 individuals of European ancestry from the UK Biobank.</p><p>
Beverages were divided into a bitter-tasting group and a sweet-tasting group.&nbsp;</p><p>
Bitter included coffee and tea, grapefruit juice, red wine and liquor; while sweet included sugar-sweetened drinks, artificially sweetened drinks and non-grapefruit juices.&nbsp;</p><p>
The researchers then performed a genome-wide association study of the consumption patterns, which were in turn validated in three US populations.</p><p>
&ldquo;Taste may be a factor,&rdquo; Cornelis says, but &ldquo;it&rsquo;s an acquired taste.&rdquo;</p><p>
Coffee, because of its bitterness, &ldquo;is something which we should be avoiding on an evolutionary level,&rdquo; she explains. &ldquo;But we&rsquo;re consuming it because we learned to equate the taste with the caffeine effect.&rdquo;</p><p>
One factor the researchers did not account for, she said, was sugar and cream that participants may have added to their coffee to conceal the bitterness.</p><p>
Cornelis also found that people who had a particular variant in a gene called the FTO preferred sugar-sweetened drinks. The result was surprising because the same variant has previously been linked to a lower risk of obesity.</p><p>
Calling the finding &ldquo;counterintuitive&rdquo;, Cornelis says: &ldquo;FTO has been something of a mystery gene, and we don&rsquo;t know exactly how it&rsquo;s linked to obesity.&nbsp;</p><p>
&ldquo;It likely plays a role in behaviour, which would be linked to weight management.&rdquo;</p><p>
The study&rsquo;s overall findings could eventually help researchers find ways to intervene when consumption patterns become unhealthy.</p><p>
Sugary beverages are linked to obesity and a variety of related conditions. Alcohol, meanwhile, is related to more than 200 diseases and responsible for about six per cent of global deaths.</p><p>
